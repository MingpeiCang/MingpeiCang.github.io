## Problem
Closed-cell plate lattices are excellent lightweight candidates, but they are difficult to manufacture because enclosed cavities trap residual powders/resins during additive manufacturing. Perforation helps manufacturability, yet poorly placed holes can degrade strength. In low-density regimes, buckling governs failure, so the central challenge is to improve manufacturability **without compromising buckling resistance**.

## Method
This work proposes a buckling-driven hole-placement strategy based on a Rayleigh quotient criterion. Instead of placing holes heuristically, optimized micro-hole locations are identified through:

- theoretical analysis of the critical buckling condition,
- linear buckling finite-element eigenmodes,
- nonlinear post-buckling analysis,
- and experimental validation after micro-LPBF fabrication.

The method is demonstrated on three representative topologies (SC, BCC, FCC) and validated using SS316L specimens.

## Key results

- Optimized perforated plate lattices achieved **higher critical buckling stress** than unperforated counterparts at the same relative density.
- Post-buckling simulations showed **comparable post-buckling compressive strength** between optimized perforated and unperforated designs.
- Micro-LPBF fabrication and characterization (μCT, SEM) confirmed good manufacturability and powder-removal feasibility.
- Compression tests over relative densities of **5.6%–37.1%** showed the proposed lattices deliver **superior Young’s modulus and compressive strength** versus shell and truss lattice baselines.

## Impact
This study turns perforation from a manufacturability compromise into a stability-aware design variable. It provides a practical route to **3D-printable, low-density, high-strength metallic plate lattices** for lightweight load-bearing applications where buckling is the key design constraint.
